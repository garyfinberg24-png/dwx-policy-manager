WEBBER WENTZEL · FIRM POLICY 4.2 POPIA CONSENT & LEGITIMATE INTEREST

1. PURPOSE AND SCOPE

This policy sets out the firm's approach to the lawful processing of personal information under the Protection of Personal Information Act No. 4 of 2013 ("POPIA"). It applies to all legal practitioners, paralegals, support staff and contractors of Webber Wentzel who collect, use, store or disclose personal information in the course of professional practice.

Compliance with this policy is mandatory. Breaches may be reported to the Information Regulator and may result in administrative fines of up to ZAR 10 million, civil liability, and disciplinary action under the Legal Practice Act.

1.1 Applicability

This policy applies in all circumstances in which personal information is processed in connection with firm business, including:

  1. Client matters — processing of information relating to clients, opposing parties, witnesses and third parties in the course of providing legal services;
  2. Business development — processing of information for marketing, pitching and business development purposes;
  3. Human resources — processing of information relating to partners, employees, secondees and candidates;
  4. Vendors and suppliers — processing of information exchanged with service providers, including counsel, experts and technology vendors.

2. LAWFUL BASIS FOR PROCESSING

Section 11 of POPIA requires that personal information be processed only where one of the listed lawful grounds applies. The lawful grounds available to the firm are consent, contractual necessity, compliance with a legal obligation, the legitimate interest of the firm, the legitimate interest of the data subject, and public law duty. The firm will not rely on blanket or implied consent in circumstances where an alternative lawful ground is more appropriate.

3. CONSENT

Where the firm elects to rely on consent as the lawful ground for processing, consent must be specific, informed, voluntary, and expressed. Bundled consent, pre-ticked checkboxes, and consent buried within general terms are not valid under POPIA.

3.1 Requirements for valid consent

Valid consent under this policy requires, at a minimum, that the data subject is provided with the following:

  1. The identity of the responsible party (the firm) and, where applicable, any co-responsible parties;
  2. The specific purpose for which the information is to be processed and any intended further use;
  3. The categories of information to be processed;
  4. Any intended recipients of the information, including where information will be transferred outside the Republic;
  5. The consequences of refusing to provide the information, including any effect on the service to be provided;
  6. The rights of the data subject under Chapter 3 of POPIA, including the right to withdraw consent at any time.

3.2 Withdrawal of consent

A data subject may withdraw consent at any time, subject to the firm's overriding legal obligations (including regulatory record-keeping under FICA and the Legal Practice Act). Withdrawal of consent does not affect the lawfulness of processing carried out prior to the withdrawal. The firm must provide a clear and accessible mechanism for consent withdrawal.

Where consent is withdrawn and no alternative lawful ground applies, the firm must cease processing the relevant personal information within 30 days and arrange for secure deletion or de-identification.

4. LEGITIMATE INTEREST

Section 11(1)(f) of POPIA permits the processing of personal information where it is necessary for the pursuit of the legitimate interests of the firm or a third party to whom the information is supplied, provided that such processing does not unjustifiably infringe the rights and freedoms of the data subject.

4.1 The three-part test

Before relying on legitimate interest, the responsible lawyer must conduct and document a three-part test:

  1. Purpose test — Is the firm pursuing a legitimate interest? Examples include fraud prevention, conflict checking, recovery of outstanding fees, and the effective conduct of pending or contemplated litigation.
  2. Necessity test — Is the processing necessary to achieve that purpose? If the same purpose can be achieved by less intrusive means (e.g. using de-identified information), the legitimate interest ground cannot be relied upon.
  3. Balancing test — Does the firm's legitimate interest override the rights and freedoms of the data subject, having regard to the nature of the information, the reasonable expectations of the data subject, and the potential impact on that person?

4.2 Documenting the assessment

The three-part test must be documented using the firm's Legitimate Interest Assessment ("LIA") template, retained on the matter file, and reviewed annually where processing is ongoing. The LIA must be reviewed and approved by the supervising partner before the processing commences.

4.3 When legitimate interest cannot be relied upon

Legitimate interest is not an appropriate lawful ground in the following circumstances:

  1. Processing of special personal information (health, biometric, criminal behaviour information) — see Section 26 POPIA;
  2. Processing of a child's personal information without an alternative lawful ground;
  3. Direct marketing by means of unsolicited electronic communications — see Section 69 POPIA.

5. PRACTICAL SCENARIOS

5.1 Conflict checks

Conflict checking against the firm's client database is ordinarily justifiable on the ground of legitimate interest (pursuit of compliance with professional rules of conduct and prevention of conflicts), and does not require specific consent from the proposed new client or from existing clients.

5.2 Secondee access to client information

Where a secondee is placed with a client, the firm must obtain written confirmation from the client specifying the categories of information the secondee may access and the purposes of that access. Legitimate interest alone is insufficient where the information includes special personal information.

5.3 Marketing and business development

Direct marketing to existing clients in respect of services similar to those already provided may proceed on the basis of legitimate interest, provided that an opt-out mechanism is included in each communication. Marketing to prospective clients, or in respect of services materially different to those already provided, requires prior opt-in consent.

6. RECORD KEEPING AND REVIEW

Each lawful basis relied upon must be recorded on the matter file, together with the supporting analysis (consent record or LIA, as applicable). The firm's Information Officer conducts a quarterly compliance review and reports to the Managing Partner's Committee.

This policy is reviewed annually and following any material amendment to POPIA, the Regulations, or binding guidance issued by the Information Regulator.
